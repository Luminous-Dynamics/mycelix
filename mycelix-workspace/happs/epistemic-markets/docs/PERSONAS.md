## Introduction

Epistemic Markets serves diverse humans with diverse needs. This document describes the specific people we design for—their stories, their needs, their fears, their journeys.

These are not abstract categories but composite portraits of real humans. When we make design decisions, we ask: "How would this affect Rosa? What would Kai need? Would this work for Amara?"

---

## The Core Personas

### 1. Rosa: The Curious Newcomer

**Demographics:**
- Age: 34
- Occupation: High school science teacher
- Location: São Paulo, Brazil
- Technical skill: Moderate (uses apps daily, doesn't code)

**Story:**
Rosa teaches biology and cares deeply about truth. She's watched her students struggle to distinguish reliable information from nonsense online. When she heard about prediction markets, she was intrigued—a place where people put their beliefs on the line?

She found Epistemic Markets through a podcast about "collective intelligence." The ideas resonated, but the interface was intimidating. She almost gave up three times before making her first prediction.

**Needs:**
- Clear, jargon-free explanations
- Low-stakes entry point
- Visible success stories from people like her
- Community that doesn't mock beginners
- Mobile-friendly interface (her laptop is old)

**Fears:**
- Looking foolish in front of strangers
- Losing money she can't afford
- Not understanding and making mistakes
- Being "not smart enough" for this

**Journey:**
```
Discovery → Hesitation → Tutorial → First small prediction →
Anxiety waiting for resolution → Relief at non-catastrophic outcome →
Second prediction with reasoning → Feedback from community →
Gradual confidence building → Mentoring other newcomers
```

**Design implications:**
- Welcoming onboarding with clear first steps
- Practice mode with fake stakes
- Friendly error messages that teach
- Community mentorship matching
- Celebrate "beginner's courage"

**Quote:** "I'm not here to get rich. I just want to think more clearly and help my students do the same."

### 2. Kai: The Data-Driven Professional

**Demographics:**
- Age: 28
- Occupation: Quantitative analyst at a hedge fund
- Location: Singapore
- Technical skill: Expert (codes daily, builds models)

**Story:**
Kai trades predictions for a living—but at work, it's about profit, not truth. The models optimize for alpha, not calibration. Kai is privately frustrated: being right feels secondary to being profitable.

Kai found Epistemic Markets while researching calibration techniques. The multi-dimensional stakes caught their attention—reputation and reasoning quality matter here, not just money. This feels more honest.

But Kai is also suspicious. Every prediction market they've seen has been gamed. They're watching carefully for manipulation before fully committing.

**Needs:**
- Detailed calibration analytics
- API access for systematic participation
- Transparency about market mechanics
- Evidence of manipulation resistance
- Competitive elements (tournaments, leaderboards)

**Fears:**
- Wasting time on a system that'll be gamed
- Missing manipulation patterns
- Being outcompeted by less scrupulous players
- Professional reputation risk if associated with "prediction markets"

**Journey:**
```
Skeptical exploration → API experimentation → Small systematic bets →
Calibration tracking → Surprise at reasoning quality of community →
Gradual trust building → Larger participation → Contributing to mechanism design
```

**Design implications:**
- Robust API with comprehensive documentation
- Detailed analytics dashboard
- Transparent market maker mechanics
- Anti-manipulation evidence and reporting
- Pseudonymous participation option

**Quote:** "I've seen what happens when prediction markets get captured. Show me this one is different."

### 3. Amara: The Domain Expert

**Demographics:**
- Age: 52
- Occupation: Epidemiologist at university
- Location: Nairobi, Kenya
- Technical skill: Moderate (uses specialized software, not a generalist)

**Story:**
Amara has spent 25 years studying disease transmission. During the pandemic, she watched as public discourse was dominated by people who'd never read an epidemiology textbook. Her expertise felt invisible.

A colleague mentioned Epistemic Markets—a place where domain expertise is tracked and weighted. Amara was skeptical (she's seen "expert" systems fail), but the MATL scoring intrigued her. Could this actually surface real expertise?

She's testing it with predictions in her domain, curious whether her track record will eventually speak for itself.

**Needs:**
- Domain-specific reputation building
- Credentialing integration (verify her actual expertise)
- Access to domain-relevant markets
- Connection with other domain experts
- Recognition of expertise without requiring constant participation

**Fears:**
- Being drowned out by confident non-experts
- Having to "prove" expertise she's spent decades building
- Academic reputation risk
- Time investment not being worth it

**Journey:**
```
Skeptical entry → Domain-specific predictions with detailed reasoning →
Watching calibration metrics → Connecting with other experts →
Seeing expertise actually weighted → Becoming domain oracle →
Mentoring next generation of experts
```

**Design implications:**
- Domain reputation that persists and compounds
- Credential verification integration
- Expert-level market creation tools
- Citation and attribution systems
- Efficient participation for busy experts

**Quote:** "I don't need validation. I need my expertise to matter when it matters."

### 4. Jordan: The Philosophical Seeker

**Demographics:**
- Age: 41
- Occupation: Philosophy professor (epistemology)
- Location: Portland, Oregon, USA
- Technical skill: Low (prefers analog, suspicious of tech)

**Story:**
Jordan has spent a career thinking about how we know things. Prediction markets are fascinating epistemically—a mechanism for aggregating beliefs. But Jordan has deep concerns about commodifying belief.

Jordan found Epistemic Markets through an academic paper on "epistemic infrastructure." The philosophical depth surprised them—this isn't just about betting; it's about cultivating epistemic virtue. The wisdom seeds concept particularly resonated.

Jordan is participating as a researcher and critic, not just a predictor. They want to understand whether this actually improves collective epistemics or just gamifies belief.

**Needs:**
- Philosophical coherence in design
- Access to meta-level discussions
- Ability to participate as researcher/observer
- Non-monetary stake options
- Documentation of epistemic principles

**Fears:**
- Reducing belief to commerce
- Incentivizing performance over authenticity
- Missing crucial philosophical problems
- Tech solutionism ignoring human complexity

**Journey:**
```
Intellectual curiosity → Deep documentation dive → Critical questions →
Experimental participation → Philosophical papers analyzing the system →
Contributing to theoretical foundations → Elder Council membership
```

**Design implications:**
- Philosophical documentation accessible
- Research API and data access
- Meta-markets about the system itself
- Non-monetary participation paths
- Welcoming of constructive criticism

**Quote:** "I'm here to understand, not just to participate. Maybe those aren't different things."

### 5. Marcus: The Community Builder

**Demographics:**
- Age: 38
- Occupation: Nonprofit director (local food systems)
- Location: Detroit, Michigan, USA
- Technical skill: Moderate (community organizing tools)

**Story:**
Marcus runs a nonprofit focused on urban agriculture. His community makes collective decisions all the time—where to plant, what to grow, when to harvest. These decisions involve real uncertainty and collective knowledge.

He discovered Epistemic Markets while looking for better decision-making tools. Traditional voting felt inadequate—it doesn't capture confidence levels or expertise differences. Prediction markets might help his community surface and aggregate their distributed knowledge.

He's not interested in the global platform as much as using the tools for his local community.

**Needs:**
- Private/community-specific markets
- Simple interface for non-technical users
- Mobile-first design
- Low barrier for community members
- Integration with existing community tools

**Fears:**
- Technology excluding community members
- Complexity overwhelming real purpose
- Privacy concerns for community data
- Dependency on external platform

**Journey:**
```
Tool exploration → Private community market experiment →
Training community members → Seeing improved decisions →
Iterating on local use → Sharing learnings with other communities →
Becoming advocate for community-scale epistemic tools
```

**Design implications:**
- Self-hosted or community-controlled options
- Simple mode for basic use cases
- Privacy controls for sensitive communities
- Import/export for community data
- Community market templates

**Quote:** "I don't care about global prediction markets. I care about my neighbors making better decisions together."

### 6. Yuki: The Next Generation

**Demographics:**
- Age: 19
- Occupation: College student (undeclared major)
- Location: Tokyo, Japan
- Technical skill: High (digital native, codes as hobby)

**Story:**
Yuki grew up online. They've seen information ecosystems collapse into tribalism. They don't trust traditional media, but they also don't trust viral content. Everything feels manipulated.

Yuki found Epistemic Markets through a Discord server about "post-truth solutions." The calibration gamification appealed to them—it feels like a game where the meta is actually being honest. Novel concept.

They're participating intensely, treating it like a competitive game where the score is calibration. They're also streaming their predictions and building a following of other young predictors.

**Needs:**
- Fast, responsive interface
- Social features (following, sharing, streaming)
- Competitive elements
- Mobile-native experience
- Cultural relevance and memes

**Fears:**
- Being lectured by older generations
- Boring, corporate interface
- Slow and clunky UX
- Privacy violations

**Journey:**
```
Discord discovery → Immediately active → Streaming predictions →
Building following → Creating prediction challenges →
Learning calibration deeply → Becoming voice for next generation →
Contributing to interface design
```

**Design implications:**
- Modern, fast UI/UX
- Social graph and following
- Share-friendly prediction formats
- Gamification done right
- Youth-inclusive governance

**Quote:** "Finally, a place where being honest is the optimal strategy. That's actually sick."

### 7. Elena: The Institutional Representative

**Demographics:**
- Age: 45
- Occupation: Chief Strategy Officer at a research foundation
- Location: Geneva, Switzerland
- Technical skill: Moderate (executive level, uses but doesn't build)

**Story:**
Elena's foundation funds research across many domains. Deciding what to fund requires predicting which research directions will be fruitful—predictions that are usually made by committee, with all the biases that implies.

She's exploring whether Epistemic Markets could help her foundation make better funding decisions. She needs enterprise features, compliance, and institutional controls—but she also wants the epistemic benefits.

**Needs:**
- Enterprise features (SSO, audit logs, compliance)
- Private institutional markets
- Integration with existing systems
- Reporting and analytics for board
- Service level agreements

**Fears:**
- Regulatory risk
- Reputational risk for foundation
- Data security concerns
- Vendor dependency

**Journey:**
```
Exploratory research → Pilot proposal to board → Legal review →
Small pilot with internal team → Evaluation and iteration →
Expanded deployment → Sharing learnings with peer institutions
```

**Design implications:**
- Enterprise tier with appropriate features
- Compliance documentation
- Security certifications
- Institutional support
- Custom deployment options

**Quote:** "We spend millions deciding what to fund. Better predictions are worth investing in."

### 8. Raj: The Skeptic

**Demographics:**
- Age: 55
- Occupation: Retired engineer, now full-time skeptic
- Location: Bangalore, India
- Technical skill: High (decades of engineering experience)

**Story:**
Raj has seen every hype cycle come and go. He was skeptical of the internet, skeptical of social media, skeptical of crypto—and was right to be skeptical in many cases. He's now skeptical of "collective intelligence" tools.

He's participating in Epistemic Markets specifically to stress-test it. He's looking for the failure modes, the manipulation vectors, the places where the theory breaks down. If he can't break it, maybe it's worth something.

**Needs:**
- Access to system details
- Ability to report vulnerabilities
- Engagement with his critiques
- Recognition for finding problems
- Evidence that problems are fixed

**Fears:**
- Being dismissed as a troll
- His concerns being ignored
- System failing after he's invested
- Being proven wrong about his skepticism

**Journey:**
```
Adversarial entry → Testing edge cases → Reporting issues →
Surprised when issues are addressed → Grudging respect →
Constructive critique → Becoming valuable red-team member
```

**Design implications:**
- Bug bounty and vulnerability reporting
- Transparent issue tracking
- Engagement with criticism
- Recognition for constructive skepticism
- Red team program

**Quote:** "If you can survive my criticism, you might be worth something."

## Anti-Personas

People we explicitly don't design for:

### The Gamer

Someone who wants to exploit the system for profit without genuine truth-seeking.

**We don't optimize for them because:** Their success would undermine the system's purpose.

**But we don't ignore them because:** They will try to participate. We design defenses.

### The Troll

Someone who participates to disrupt and cause chaos.

**We don't optimize for them because:** Their goals are antithetical to ours.

**But we don't ignore them because:** They will appear. We design moderation.

### The Authoritarian

Someone who wants to use the system to establish their version of truth.

**We don't optimize for them because:** Epistemic authoritarianism is a core anti-value.

**But we don't ignore them because:** They may try to capture the system. We design resistance.

## Persona-Driven Design Checklist

When making design decisions, ask:

| Persona | Question |
|---------|----------|
| Rosa | Can a newcomer understand and use this? |
| Kai | Does this withstand sophisticated analysis? |
| Amara | Does this respect and surface expertise? |
| Jordan | Is this philosophically coherent? |
| Marcus | Does this work for communities, not just individuals? |
| Yuki | Is this engaging for digital natives? |
| Elena | Does this meet institutional requirements? |
| Raj | Can this survive adversarial testing? |

## Accessibility Across Personas

### Language
- Rosa, Marcus: Needs clear, simple language
- Kai, Amara: Can handle technical language in their domain
- Jordan: Appreciates precise philosophical language
- Yuki: Expects contemporary, casual communication

### Technical Skill
- Jordan, Rosa: Need more guidance
- Kai, Yuki, Raj: Can handle complex features
- Marcus, Amara: Domain expertise != technical expertise

### Time Availability
- Elena, Amara: Very limited time
- Yuki, Raj: More time available
- Marcus, Rosa: Moderate but fragmented time

### Stakes Comfort
- Elena, Kai: Comfortable with significant stakes
- Rosa, Marcus: Need low-stakes options
- Jordan, Raj: Prefer non-monetary stakes

## Persona Evolution

Personas change over time:

**Rosa's evolution:**
Year 1: Nervous newcomer making small predictions
Year 2: Confident participant mentoring others
Year 3: Community leader creating markets for education domain

**Kai's evolution:**
Year 1: Skeptical analyst testing the system
Year 2: Active participant building prediction models
Year 3: Mechanism design contributor improving the system

**Personas aren't static.** Design for growth.

---

## Creating New Personas

As the system grows, new personas will emerge. When creating new personas:

1. **Ground in research** - Interview real users
2. **Be specific** - Name, age, location, story
3. **Include fears** - What might drive them away?
4. **Map the journey** - From discovery to mastery
5. **Extract implications** - What does this mean for design?
6. **Test against existing** - How do they interact with other personas?

These eight personas represent the diversity of humans we serve. They are not the only users—they are lenses through which we see the human implications of our design choices.

When we design for Rosa's courage, Kai's rigor, Amara's expertise, Jordan's philosophy, Marcus's community, Yuki's energy, Elena's institution, and Raj's skepticism—we design for humanity.
