## Part III: Design Choice Analysis

### Why Multi-Dimensional Stakes?

**Existing approach:** Money or reputation (not both)

**Our approach:** Money + reputation + social + commitment + time + legacy

**Rationale:**
- Money isn't the only valuable thing
- Different stakes attract different participants
- Expertise can be staked without wealth
- Social stakes create accountability networks
- Commitment stakes align belief and action
- Legacy stakes connect generations

**Evidence:**
- Research on intrinsic vs. extrinsic motivation
- Desire for non-monetary participation
- Community-building through social stakes

---

### Why Reasoning Requirements?

**Existing approach:** Reasoning optional or absent

**Our approach:** Reasoning required for serious predictions

**Rationale:**
- Reasoning is more valuable than predictions
- Forces articulation improves thinking
- Creates learning resources
- Enables quality assessment
- Builds intellectual community

**Trade-offs:**
- Higher friction for casual participation
- Requires literacy and time
- May exclude some participants

**Mitigation:**
- Progressive requirements (more reasoning for higher stakes)
- Templates and prompts to lower barrier
- Voice-to-text options

---

### Why Distributed Oracles?

**Existing approach:** Centralized resolution or simple token voting

**Our approach:** MATL-weighted distributed oracle network

**Rationale:**
- No single point of failure
- Expertise-weighted resolution
- Byzantine fault tolerance
- Manipulation resistance
- Trust through transparency

**Trade-offs:**
- More complex resolution process
- Slower for simple questions
- Bootstrap challenges

**Mitigation:**
- Multiple resolution mechanisms by question type
- Expedited paths for clear outcomes
- Gradual trust building

---

### Why Wisdom Seeds?

**Existing approach:** Comments or nothing

**Our approach:** Structured wisdom seeds with future rewards

**Rationale:**
- Knowledge should accumulate
- Future generations matter
- Incentivize long-term thinking
- Create learning resources
- Build intergenerational connection

**Trade-offs:**
- Reward timing is distant
- Quality assessment is hard
- May not be valued initially

**Mitigation:**
- Near-term recognition
- Community curation
- Elder rewards

---

### Why Decentralized Governance?

**Existing approach:** Company control

**Our approach:** Protocol-level community governance

**Rationale:**
- Aligned with epistemic values
- Resistant to capture
- Community ownership
- Evolutionary capacity
- Legitimacy through participation

**Trade-offs:**
- Slower decision-making
- Governance overhead
- Potential gridlock

**Mitigation:**
- Layered governance
- Delegation options
- Emergency procedures

## Part VIII: The Honest Assessment

### Where we may be wrong:

**Complexity hypothesis:**
Maybe simpler is actually better. Perhaps multi-dimensional stakes and reasoning requirements create too much friction without proportional benefit.

**Wisdom hypothesis:**
Maybe wisdom doesn't actually accumulate. Perhaps each generation has to relearn everything regardless.

**Governance hypothesis:**
Maybe decentralized governance is too slow to be practical. Perhaps benevolent dictatorships are more effective.

**Integration hypothesis:**
Maybe standalone markets are actually better. Perhaps integration creates dependencies without benefits.

### How we'll find out:

- Build it
- Measure outcomes
- Compare to alternatives
- Update based on evidence

## Conclusion

We are not claiming to be better than all alternatives. We are claiming to be different in specific, intentional ways that serve our values:

- **Truth-seeking** over profit-maximizing
- **Multi-dimensional** over single-dimensional
- **Integrated** over standalone
- **Community-governed** over platform-controlled
- **Long-term** over short-term

Whether these choices are correct is itself a prediction. We're betting on it.
